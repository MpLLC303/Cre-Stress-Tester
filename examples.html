Three real critiques, three different deal types — so you can see what the output actually looks like before you run your own.

Example 01 · Aggressive · Multifamily

Value-Add Apartment Deal in Phoenix

A 284-unit garden-style apartment complex built in 1986. The sponsor is raising money to buy it, renovate the interiors, and sell in five years. The pitch projects 18.2% returns. The tool flagged seven assumptions that make the deal more fragile than the pitch suggests.

What the tool caught
Unrealistic Rent Growth

The pitch assumes rents will grow 5.75% every year for five years. Historical averages for this submarket run around 3%. Sustaining 5.75% requires the market to outperform its long-run average by 275 basis points annually.

Exit Cap Compression

The pitch assumes the building will sell at a tighter valuation than it was bought at — a bet that interest rates fall or the building improves relative to its peers. A flat exit cap reduces projected returns from 18.2% to roughly 13–14%.

Interest-Only Loan Masks Refinance Risk

The loan requires interest-only payments for all five years. This inflates year-one cash flow but leaves no principal paid down at sale. If interest rates are higher at exit, the refinance math breaks.

Operating Expense Growth Understated

The pitch models expenses growing 1.5% annually. Real inflation for property operations — especially insurance and maintenance — typically runs 3–4%. The gap compounds into $130–200K of understated annual expenses by year five.

Why this matters This pitch isn't fraudulent — it's optimistic. Each assumption, individually, is defensible on its most charitable reading. Stacked together, they inflate projected returns by 400–500 basis points. An experienced analyst would walk into committee and ask for downside cases on every number.
Example 02 · Control Case · Industrial

Modern Logistics Facility in the Inland Empire

A 445,000 square foot warehouse built in 2019, currently 85% leased to two tenants. The pitch projects a 12.3% return over seven years. Most of the underwriting is defensible — but four minor items deserve pressure-testing.

The tool is designed to recognize when a deal is sound. Over-flagging a defensible deal is just as bad as missing real red flags. The four items below are framed as pressure-tests, not kill criteria.
Minor items worth pressure-testing
Comp Set Leans on 2021 Trades

The comparable sales cited in the pitch all traded in 2021–2022, when cap rates were historically tight. Recalculating exit valuation against more recent trades would give a more conservative estimate.

Rent Growth at the Top of Market Range

4.5% annual rent growth is at the upper bound of defensible — the memo acknowledges this. Worth running a downside case at 3% to understand sensitivity.

Replacement Reserves Slightly Thin

$0.10 per square foot is below the typical institutional range of $0.15–$0.20. Not a deal-breaker — worth confirming against the lender's requirement.

Why this matters A tool that flags everything as a red flag is just noise. This deal is mostly solid. The pressure-tests let you interrogate the specific spots where reasonable people could disagree — which is how good due diligence actually works.
Example 03 · Sector Headwinds · Office

Trophy Office Tower in Midtown Manhattan

A 680,000 square foot Class A office building in Midtown East, being acquired at a 31% discount to its 2019 appraised value. The pitch frames this as a defensive entry basis. The tool identified structural risks the basis discount does not offset.

What the tool caught
Lease Rollover Concentration

38% of the building's leases expire in years 4–6 of the hold period — including a single 125,000 square foot technology tenant in year 5. If that tenant downsizes or leaves, the building's projected cash flow breaks.

Concessions Treated as Temporary

Recent leases include multiple years of free rent. The pitch assumes these concessions burn off by year three and rents reset to stated contract levels. In today's office market, concessions are persistent — this optimism inflates projected income on 27% of the building.

Exit Valuation Depends on Rates Falling

The pitch ties its exit valuation to a specific forecast of interest rates declining by year 10. If rates hold at current levels, exit proceeds fall 10–20% — turning a target-return deal into a below-target outcome.

Why this matters Office is the hardest asset class to underwrite right now. A basis discount — even a significant one — only helps if the underlying assumptions are sound. This pitch stacks multiple sector-specific risks that compound with each other. The tool's job isn't to say the deal is bad; it's to make the risks explicit so you can decide.
