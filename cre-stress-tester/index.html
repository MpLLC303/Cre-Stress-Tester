About This Project

A student project that turned into a working tool.

§ 01 — The Origin

Where this came from.

I'm Micah Price, an undergraduate at the University of Colorado Boulder's Leeds School of Business, currently enrolled in BCOR 2205 — Introduction to Information Management and Analytics. The assignment that started this was to build a Custom GPT around any topic I chose. Most of my classmates built general-purpose chatbots. I wanted to build something an acquisitions analyst at a mid-market real estate private equity firm might actually open on their second monitor.

The result is this tool. It started as a simple Custom GPT configuration and grew into a full-stack web application as I wanted more control over the knowledge base, the output format, and the guardrails against hallucination. What you're using now is the production version — deployed on Vercel, backed by the Anthropic Claude API, and grounded in a curated six-file knowledge base built from public industry research.

§ 02 — What This Project Demonstrates

The capabilities this project evidences.

This is both a portfolio piece and a practice artifact. The capabilities it demonstrates:

  • Applied prompt engineering — a deliberate ten-technique stack, visible in the production system prompt and versioned through three measurable iterations.
  • Curated knowledge base construction — six reference files assembled from public CBRE, NAREIT, NCREIF, Green Street, and NAIOP research, with explicit verification gates before upload.
  • Full-stack deployment — HTML/CSS/JS frontend, Vercel serverless backend, Anthropic Claude API integration, rate limiting, error handling.
  • Cost-aware model selection — benchmarked Sonnet 4.6 against Haiku 4.5 on a four-persona test set; made an explicit tradeoff based on measured quality and latency data.
  • Test-driven iteration — four test personas with known answer keys, five-dimension scoring rubric, including a deliberate control case to measure over-flagging behavior.
§ 03 — What I'm Building Toward

Target roles.

I'm building toward internships and early-career roles in REIT acquisitions, private equity real estate, and corporate development. This tool is designed to speak directly to those audiences — it captures the kind of first-pass analytical thinking that distinguishes a junior analyst worth keeping from one who just runs numbers.

§ Build Details

How This Was Built

A look under the hood — the architecture, the prompt engineering, the tradeoffs, the testing protocol.

§ 01 — Architecture

A full-stack application with a curated knowledge base.

The tool is a single-page web application hosted on Vercel. The frontend is vanilla HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — no frameworks, no build step. The backend is a Node.js serverless function that receives the submitted memo, loads the six knowledge files, and calls the Anthropic Claude API with a production-hardened system prompt. The structured XML response is parsed and rendered into visual sections.

§ 02 — Prompt Engineering Stack

Ten proven techniques, applied across every prompt.

The system prompt governing the tool is built on a deliberate stack of ten prompt engineering techniques. Each one prevents a specific failure mode — hallucination, over-flagging, generic critique, sycophancy, response bloat. The production prompt combines seven of the ten in a single instruction set.

role priming xml delimiters output schemas few-shot examples chain-of-thought negative constraints self-critique loops confidence calibration length caps refusal anchors

The most important of these for this tool are refusal anchors and confidence calibration — these are what prevent the tool from making up market data or giving you the "yes this is a great deal" response that undermines stress-testing. Every time the tool doesn't know a specific number, it says so and points to a verifiable source instead of guessing.

§ 03 — Three-Version Iteration

The system prompt was rebuilt twice.

The production version (v3) is the result of two visible iteration steps. Each version was tested against the same four test cases to measure the improvement.

Version 01

Baseline

Role priming only. No knowledge files. Generic critique with frequent hallucination. Deliberately weak to establish a measurable starting point.

Version 02

Knowledge Files

Added the six curated knowledge files. Added a structured intake. Added negative constraints on marketing language. Material improvement in citation discipline.

Version 03

Production

Seven explicit rules, mandatory XML output schema, refusal anchors, chain-of-thought preamble, mandatory disclaimer. This is the version running in production.

§ 04 — Model Selection & Cost

Sonnet 4.6 vs. Haiku 4.5 — a real engineering tradeoff.

I benchmarked Claude Sonnet 4.6 against Claude Haiku 4.5 across the four-persona test set. Sonnet catches roughly 90% of embedded red flags; Haiku catches about 70% but responds in under 10 seconds. For the demo-heavy workload of a portfolio tool, Haiku's speed is the right tradeoff.

Prompt caching on the knowledge base means every call after the first gets a 90% discount on input tokens, because the six knowledge files are static across calls. The effective per-analysis cost is under two cents.

§ 05 — The Knowledge Base

Six curated files, roughly 10,000 tokens of grounded reference.

The knowledge base was assembled from public industry sources — CBRE Research, NAREIT T-Tracker, NCREIF, Green Street Advisors, NAIOP, and Adventures in CRE. Every numeric range in the files is flagged with a [VERIFY] tag and was sanity-checked against the published data before upload.

  1. Fundamentals Reference — pro forma structure, core return metrics, investment strategy definitions, capital stack basics.
  2. Red Flag Catalog — 25 named patterns across five categories: revenue, expense, cap rate & exit, debt & capital structure, structural & market.
  3. Asset Class Market Ranges — institutional underwriting ranges by asset class (multifamily, office, industrial, retail, hospitality).
  4. Sensitivity Framework — the hierarchy of assumption sensitivity and how to communicate fragility quantitatively.
  5. Sample Annotated Memos — one defensible memo and one weak memo, with red flags cited by name.
  6. Cap Rate & Cycle Reference — historical cap rate context and the Treasury-yield relationship.
§ 06 — Testing Protocol

Four test cases, five scoring dimensions, one control.

Four test personas were constructed with known answer keys — each a fictional but realistic deal memo with a specific number of embedded red flags. The tool was scored on five dimensions: red flag detection, citation discipline, fragility analysis, no-hallucination compliance, and analytical depth.

Persona C is a deliberate control case. It contains only four minor concerns, and the deal is mostly defensible. The test isn't just "can the tool catch red flags?" — it's also "can the tool recognize when there aren't any?" A tool that flags everything is as useless as one that flags nothing. This matters more than it sounds.

§ 07 — Honest Limitations

What this tool can't do.

Limitations

The tool cannot verify claims in the pitch document — if a memo says rent grew 5% last year, the tool cannot check whether that happened. It flags assumptions that look unrealistic against industry norms. The tool does not have live market data, so it refuses to quote current cap rates or loan spreads. It is not a substitute for due diligence. It is not investment advice. The quality of its critique is limited by the quality of the submitted memo.

§ Examples

See the Tool in Action

Three real critiques, three different deal types — so you can see what the output actually looks like before you run your own.

Example 01 · Aggressive · Multifamily

Value-Add Apartment Deal in Phoenix

A 284-unit garden-style apartment complex built in 1986. The sponsor is raising money to buy it, renovate the interiors, and sell in five years. The pitch projects 18.2% returns. The tool flagged seven assumptions that make the deal more fragile than the pitch suggests.

What the tool caught
Unrealistic Rent Growth

The pitch assumes rents will grow 5.75% every year for five years. Historical averages for this submarket run around 3%. Sustaining 5.75% requires the market to outperform its long-run average by 275 basis points annually.

Exit Cap Compression

The pitch assumes the building will sell at a tighter valuation than it was bought at — a bet that interest rates fall or the building improves relative to its peers. A flat exit cap reduces projected returns from 18.2% to roughly 13–14%.

Interest-Only Loan Masks Refinance Risk

The loan requires interest-only payments for all five years. This inflates year-one cash flow but leaves no principal paid down at sale. If interest rates are higher at exit, the refinance math breaks.

Operating Expense Growth Understated

The pitch models expenses growing 1.5% annually. Real inflation for property operations — especially insurance and maintenance — typically runs 3–4%. The gap compounds into $130–200K of understated annual expenses by year five.

Why this matters This pitch isn't fraudulent — it's optimistic. Each assumption, individually, is defensible on its most charitable reading. Stacked together, they inflate projected returns by 400–500 basis points. An experienced analyst would walk into committee and ask for downside cases on every number.
Example 02 · Control Case · Industrial

Modern Logistics Facility in the Inland Empire

A 445,000 square foot warehouse built in 2019, currently 85% leased to two tenants. The pitch projects a 12.3% return over seven years. Most of the underwriting is defensible — but four minor items deserve pressure-testing.

The tool is designed to recognize when a deal is sound. Over-flagging a defensible deal is just as bad as missing real red flags. The four items below are framed as pressure-tests, not kill criteria.
Minor items worth pressure-testing
Comp Set Leans on 2021 Trades

The comparable sales cited in the pitch all traded in 2021–2022, when cap rates were historically tight. Recalculating exit valuation against more recent trades would give a more conservative estimate.

Rent Growth at the Top of Market Range

4.5% annual rent growth is at the upper bound of defensible — the memo acknowledges this. Worth running a downside case at 3% to understand sensitivity.

Replacement Reserves Slightly Thin

$0.10 per square foot is below the typical institutional range of $0.15–$0.20. Not a deal-breaker — worth confirming against the lender's requirement.

Why this matters A tool that flags everything as a red flag is just noise. This deal is mostly solid. The pressure-tests let you interrogate the specific spots where reasonable people could disagree — which is how good due diligence actually works.
Example 03 · Sector Headwinds · Office

Trophy Office Tower in Midtown Manhattan

A 680,000 square foot Class A office building in Midtown East, being acquired at a 31% discount to its 2019 appraised value. The pitch frames this as a defensive entry basis. The tool identified structural risks the basis discount does not offset.

What the tool caught
Lease Rollover Concentration

38% of the building's leases expire in years 4–6 of the hold period — including a single 125,000 square foot technology tenant in year 5. If that tenant downsizes or leaves, the building's projected cash flow breaks.

Concessions Treated as Temporary

Recent leases include multiple years of free rent. The pitch assumes these concessions burn off by year three and rents reset to stated contract levels. In today's office market, concessions are persistent — this optimism inflates projected income on 27% of the building.

Exit Valuation Depends on Rates Falling

The pitch ties its exit valuation to a specific forecast of interest rates declining by year 10. If rates hold at current levels, exit proceeds fall 10–20% — turning a target-return deal into a below-target outcome.

Why this matters Office is the hardest asset class to underwrite right now. A basis discount — even a significant one — only helps if the underlying assumptions are sound. This pitch stacks multiple sector-specific risks that compound with each other. The tool's job isn't to say the deal is bad; it's to make the risks explicit so you can decide.
