§ 01 — What is this thing?

In plain English, this tool reads pitch documents and tells you what to worry about.

When someone wants to buy an apartment building, an office tower, or a warehouse, they usually write a pitch document to raise money from investors. Those documents are packed with projections — how much rent will grow, what the building will be worth in five years, how much it costs to run. Some of those projections are reasonable. Some are quietly too optimistic.

Spotting the weak assumptions is a skill that takes years to build. Experienced real estate analysts can read a ten-page pitch and, within a few minutes, point at the three numbers that don't add up. This tool captures that skill and makes it available in fifteen seconds.

You paste the document. The tool reads it, checks each assumption against a curated knowledge base of how these documents usually fail, and sends back a structured critique — concerns named in plain language, ranked by how much money is at stake, and with a specific list of questions to bring back to whoever wrote the document.

One honest caveat This tool critiques the writing. It cannot verify claims — if the document says rent grew 5% last year, the tool will not tell you whether that is true. It flags assumptions that look unrealistic against industry norms. You still need to check the facts.
§ 02 — What makes it useful

Four things this tool does that a generic chatbot will not.

Feature 01
Catches 25 common pitfalls
A curated catalog of the most frequent mistakes in real estate pitch documents — organized by category so every concern has a name, not just a vague gut feeling.
Feature 02
Grounded in real sources
Built on public research from the industry's most cited firms — CBRE, NAREIT, NCREIF, and Green Street. The tool compares your document against how real institutional investors think.
Feature 03
Refuses to make things up
Seven explicit rules prevent the tool from inventing market data. If it doesn't know the current cap rate for a specific city, it says so and tells you where to look — it will never guess a number.
Feature 04
Shows its work
Every concern is named, explained in plain language, and quantified where possible (what happens to projected returns if the assumption is wrong). You get reasoning, not vibes.
