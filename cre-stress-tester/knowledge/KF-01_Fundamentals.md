# CRE Underwriting Fundamentals Reference

## 1. Pro Forma Structure (NOI Walk)

- **Gross Potential Rent (GPR)** — Total rent if every unit/sf were leased at market rate for the full year. *Example: 200 units × $1,800/mo × 12 = $4,320,000.*
- **Loss to Lease** — Gap between in-place contract rent and current market rent. *Example: ($4,320,000 GPR – $4,150,000 in-place) = $170,000 loss to lease.*
- **Vacancy Loss** — Rent forgone on physically empty units. *Example: 5% vacancy on $4,320,000 = $216,000.*
- **Concessions** — Free rent, move-in credits, or other incentives netted from rent. *Example: One month free on 30 new leases = $54,000.*
- **Bad Debt / Credit Loss** — Uncollected rent from delinquent tenants. *Example: 1.0% of GPR = $43,200.*
- **Effective Gross Income (EGI)** — GPR minus the four deductions above plus Other Income (parking, laundry, RUBS, fees). *Example: $4,320,000 – $483,200 + $180,000 Other Income = $4,016,800.*
- **Operating Expenses (Opex)** — Property taxes, insurance, utilities, repairs, payroll, management fee, G&A. Excludes debt service and capex.
- **Net Operating Income (NOI)** — EGI minus Opex. NOI is the cash flow figure used to value the asset. *Example: $4,016,800 EGI – $1,650,000 Opex = $2,366,800 NOI.*

## 2. Core Return Metrics

- **Cap Rate = NOI ÷ Purchase Price.** Used for unlevered valuation and comparison across deals. A lower cap rate signals higher price per dollar of NOI (more investor demand or perceived growth); a higher cap rate signals more risk or weaker demand. Cap rates are asset-class and market-cycle dependent [VERIFY].
- **Cash-on-Cash Return = Annual Pre-Tax Cash Flow ÷ Total Equity Invested.** Measures levered cash yield in a single year. Highly sensitive to leverage: more debt typically raises cash-on-cash but also raises risk. Institutional core deals often target 5–7% [VERIFY]; value-add stabilized year often targets 8%+ [VERIFY].
- **IRR (Internal Rate of Return)** — The discount rate that sets the NPV of all deal cash flows (initial equity outlay, interim distributions, sale proceeds) to zero. Timing of cash flows materially affects IRR — earlier distributions and earlier sales raise IRR, all else equal.
- **Equity Multiple = Total Equity Distributions ÷ Total Equity Invested.** Measures total dollars returned per dollar invested, ignoring time. Targets by strategy [VERIFY]: Core ~1.4–1.7x, Value-Add ~1.8–2.2x, Opportunistic 2.0x+.
- **Yield-on-Cost = Stabilized NOI ÷ Total Project Cost (Purchase + Capex).** Matters more than cap rate on value-add and development because it measures return on the all-in basis. The spread between Yield-on-Cost and market cap rate is the "development spread" [VERIFY].

## 3. Investment Strategy Definitions

- **Core** — Stabilized, institutional-quality assets in primary markets. Leverage 40–55% LTV [VERIFY]. Target IRR 7–10% [VERIFY]. Hold 7–10+ years. Income-focused, low risk.
- **Core-Plus** — Stabilized assets with light value-creation upside (lease-up, mild reposition). Leverage 50–65% LTV [VERIFY]. Target IRR 9–12% [VERIFY]. Hold 5–10 years. Modest risk.
- **Value-Add** — Underperforming assets requiring renovation, lease-up, or operational improvement. Leverage 60–75% LTV [VERIFY]. Target IRR 13–18% [VERIFY]. Hold 3–7 years. Equity multiple usually carries more weight than current yield.
- **Opportunistic** — Development, major repositioning, distressed, or niche strategies. Leverage 65–80%+ LTC [VERIFY]. Target IRR 18%+ [VERIFY]. Hold 3–7 years. Highest risk; outcomes depend on execution and market timing.

## 4. Hold Period and Exit Math

- **Exit Value = Year-of-Sale NOI ÷ Exit Cap Rate.** Sale proceeds dominate IRR on most 5- to 10-year holds because the lump-sum exit typically dwarfs interim cash flows in present value terms.
- **Why Exit Cap Dominates** — Because exit value = NOI ÷ cap, small changes in the exit cap rate produce large dollar swings in proceeds. A 25 bps move in exit cap can shift IRR by ~150–200 bps on a 5-year hold [VERIFY], making exit cap the single most sensitive line item in most underwriting.
- **Going-In vs. Exit Cap Spread (Rule of Thumb)** — Default conservative practice is Exit Cap ≥ Going-In Cap. The "asset-aging" argument: a building five years older sells at the same or wider cap than today's buyer paid. Tightening the exit cap below the going-in cap requires explicit justification (e.g., NOI growth from value-add execution that demonstrably re-tiers the asset).

## 5. Sources of Capital and Capital Stack

The capital stack is ordered by repayment priority, senior to junior:

- **Senior Debt** — First-lien mortgage. Lowest cost, paid first, secured by the property. Typical institutional LTV 55–70% depending on asset class and lender [VERIFY].
- **Mezzanine Debt** — Subordinate debt secured by equity interests rather than the property itself. Higher coupon than senior. Sits between senior debt and equity.
- **Preferred Equity** — Equity with priority on distributions and capital return ahead of common equity. May carry a fixed preferred return and conversion features.
- **Common Equity** — Last to be repaid, first to absorb losses. Receives all upside above debt service and preferred returns.

**LTV (Loan-to-Value) = Loan Amount ÷ Appraised Value.** Used at acquisition or refinance of stabilized assets.
**LTC (Loan-to-Cost) = Loan Amount ÷ Total Project Cost.** Used for value-add and development where cost basis differs materially from value.
